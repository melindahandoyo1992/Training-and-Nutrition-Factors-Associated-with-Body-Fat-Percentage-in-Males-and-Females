# Training-and-Nutrition-Factors-Associated-with-Body-Fat-Percentage-in-Males-and-Females

## Project overview

This project analyzes observational fitness and nutrition data to identify lifestyle patterns associated with healthier body fat percentages among adults aged 40–55. Training and nutrition variables were analyzed separately for male and female participants, and the findings were combined to identify common lifestyle characteristics associated with lower body fat percentages. Because the analysis is observational, the results identify associations rather than causal relationships.

## Brief background

Body fat percentage is one of the key indicators of overall health. As people age, particularly between 40 and 55 years old, physiological changes such as a slower metabolism, hormonal changes, and progressive muscle loss make it more difficult to lose fat and maintain a healthy body composition.
Regular exercise and appropriate nutrition are widely recognized as important lifestyle factors for maintaining a healthy body fat percentage. However, different exercise and dietary patterns may produce different outcomes across demographic groups.
This project analyzes training and nutrition patterns associated with healthy body fat percentages using observational fitness data. The goal is to identify lifestyle characteristics that are commonly associated with lower body fat percentages across different age groups and genders.

## Main question

What exercise and nutrition patterns are most associated with healthy body fat percentage ranges across demographic groups?

## Supporting questions

- Does workout frequency correlate with healthier body fat percentages?
- Which workout types are associated with lower body fat percentages?
- Is protein intake associated with body fat percentage?
- Do these relationships differ across age groups?
- Do these patterns differ between males and females?

## Key finding for female participants

Female participants aged 40–55 who performed strength training 3–4 times per week, exercised for approximately 1.3 hours per session, followed a Paleo diet, consumed approximately 0.7 g/kg/day of protein, and distributed protein intake across 2–4 meals per day were associated with the lowest average body fat percentage in this dataset.


## Recommendation for female participants

Based on the combined findings from the training and nutrition analyses, women aged 40–55 aiming to maintain a healthy body fat percentage may consider the following:
- Strength training 3–4 times per week
- Exercising for approximately 1.3 hours per session
- Following a Paleo-style diet
- Distributing protein intake across 2–4 meals per day
- Regarding protein intake: Participants consuming approximately 0.7 g/kg/day had the lowest average body fat percentage in this dataset. However, this represents an observed association rather than an optimal intake recommendation. Current sports nutrition guidelines generally recommend 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day for active adults performing resistance training to support muscle maintenance and recovery.


## Key finding for male participants

Male participants aged 40–55 who performed cardio training three times per week, exercised for approximately 1.2 hours per session, followed a low-carbohydrate diet, consumed approximately 0.7 g/kg/day of protein, and distributed protein intake across 2–4 meals per day were associated with the lowest average body fat percentage in this dataset.

## Recommendation for male participants

Based on the combined findings from the training and nutrition analyses, men aged 40–55 aiming to maintain a healthy body fat percentage may consider the following:
- Cardio training 3 times per week
- Exercising for approximately 1.2 hours per session
- Following a low carbs diet
- Distributing protein intake across 2–4 meals per day
- Regarding protein intake: Participants consuming approximately 0.7 g/kg/day had the lowest average body fat percentage in this dataset. However, this represents an observed association rather than an optimal intake recommendation. Current sports nutrition guidelines generally recommend 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day for active adults performing resistance training to support muscle maintenance and recovery.

## Limitations

- This analysis is observational and identifies associations rather than causal relationships.
- The dataset was filtered to participants with healthy body fat percentage ranges and may not generalize to all populations.
- Lifestyle factors such as sleep quality, genetics, and overall caloric intake were not included.
- Protein intake findings should not be interpreted as dietary recommendations.
